# Source evaluation

## Grades

- **A**: regulator, exchange, statutory filing, fund legal document, official statistics, formal standard.
- **B**: peer-reviewed research, authoritative textbook, transparent industry body, maintained open-source project with clear license.
- **C**: professional analysis or expert material with inspectable reasoning and conflicts disclosed.
- **D**: forums, social posts, anonymous claims, screenshots, and unverified summaries; use only to discover stronger sources.

## Expert and repository admission

Do not rank by fame, followers, recent returns, or Star count alone. Record:

1. Primary artifacts: letters, filings, books, papers, source code, reproducible notebooks.
2. Method: what is measured, decision horizon, benchmark, costs, and falsification conditions.
3. Incentives: products sold, positions, sponsorships, survivorship and selection bias.
4. Track-record quality: complete period, capacity, risk, drawdown, and whether results are audited.
5. Reusability: license, pinned version, documentation, tests, data assumptions, maintenance.
6. Limits: markets where the method fails, missing data, translation risk, and date sensitivity.

Create a source card for the artifact, not a page that asks readers to trust a personality. Extract testable ideas into concept or method pages and preserve disagreements.

## Web and copyright

- Recheck anything current or likely to change.
- Prefer canonical pages over search snippets and mirrors.
- Record `retrieved` and the rule/report version or effective date.
- Quote sparingly. Summarize and link; do not store paid reports, full books, proprietary datasets, or restricted standards.
- Treat undocumented endpoints and session authentication as fragile local adapters. Never publish Cookies, tokens, request signatures, or access-control bypasses.
